US troops in Saudi Arabia over time

A brief history and visualization of the record of US troops in the Middle East.

By Nyah Mathis

The American military presence in Saudi Arabia traces back to 1945, when the U.S. built Dhahran Airfield as a strategic stopover and Cold War outpost. A formal basing agreement kept troop numbers climbing through the late 1950s, until Saudi Arabia declined to renew the lease in 1962. A small advisory corps rarely exceeding a few hundred personnel remained, operating under the U.S. Military Training Mission and helping modernize the Saudi armed forces with American weapons systems and training through the 1970s and 1980s. That modest footprint exploded in August 1990, when Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. Operation Desert Shield poured troops into the Saudi desert almost overnight, and by the time Operation Desert Storm launched in January 1991, more than half a million coalition forces had massed in the kingdom in the largest American overseas deployment since Vietnam. Most went home after Kuwait's liberation, but thousands stayed for over a decade to enforce a no-fly zone over southern Iraq under Operation Southern Watch, sustaining elevated troop levels through a series of crises including the 1996 Khobar Towers bombing and the 1998 air campaign against Iraq. The 2003 invasion of Iraq finally ended the large-scale presence, as forces relocated to Qatar. A small training mission persisted until 2019, when Iranian attacks on Saudi oil facilities prompted the U.S. to reopen Prince Sultan Air Base and push troop numbers back toward 3,000.
