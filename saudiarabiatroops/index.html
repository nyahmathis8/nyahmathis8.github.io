US troops in Saudi Arabia over time

A brief history and visualization of the record of US troops in the Middle East.

By Nyah Mathis

American soldiers have been a fixture in Saudi Arabia since 1945, when the U.S. built an airfield near Dhahran as a refueling stop and Cold War foothold. The two countries formalized the arrangement in 1951, and troop numbers grew steadily until Saudi Arabia showed the Americans the door in 1962. A small advisory group stayed on, training Saudi forces and managing arms deals, and for the next three decades the presence hovered at a few hundred people.

Then Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait in August 1990, and everything changed. The U.S. rushed hundreds of thousands of troops into the Saudi desert in a matter of weeks, and by early 1991 more than half a million coalition forces had assembled there in the biggest American military buildup since Vietnam. The war to liberate Kuwait was over in 100 hours, but the troops did not all go home. For more than a decade, thousands of American service members stayed to patrol the skies over southern Iraq and keep Saddam in check, weathering a deadly terrorist bombing at Khobar Towers in 1996 and a four-day air campaign against Iraq in 1998. The 2003 invasion of Iraq finally brought most of them home, with remaining forces shifting to Qatar. The numbers shrank back to a skeleton crew of trainers until 2019, when Iran attacked Saudi oil facilities and the U.S. sent troops back to reopen a base it had abandoned fifteen years earlier.
